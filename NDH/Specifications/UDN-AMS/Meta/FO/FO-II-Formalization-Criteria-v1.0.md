# ⭐ **NDH‑AMS — FO‑II Formalization Criteria (v1.0)**  
### *Criteria for identifying formal structural commitment events within NDH*

---

## ⭐ 1. Definition  
An **FO‑II Formalization Event** is a deliberate structural commitment made by NDH.  
It transforms a stabilized conceptual primitive into an official, persistent component of the system’s architecture.

FO‑II marks the transition from:

- **stable concept → formal structure**

It is intentional, binding, and system‑wide.

---

## ⭐ 2. Core Criteria  
A candidate event qualifies as FO‑II when it satisfies **all** of the following criteria.

---

### **Intentional Commitment**  
The event must be the result of a deliberate decision to formalize a concept.  
Indicators include:

- explicit structural adoption  
- intentional standardization  
- purposeful integration into system behavior  
- clear commitment to persistence  

FO‑II never occurs spontaneously.

---

### **Structural Definition**  
The event must define or refine a structural element of NDH.  
This includes:

- establishing interfaces  
- defining rules or constraints  
- specifying operational guarantees  
- creating stable interaction patterns  

The structure must be precise enough to support consistent use.

---

### **System‑Wide Applicability**  
The event must affect NDH beyond a local context.  
System‑wide applicability is present when:

- multiple components rely on the structure  
- the structure influences manifold behavior  
- the commitment persists across contexts  
- the structure becomes part of NDH’s identity  

FO‑II events are not local optimizations — they are global commitments.

---

### **Persistence Across States**  
The formalized structure must remain stable across manifold transitions.  
Persistence is confirmed when:

- the structure survives altitude shifts  
- the behavior remains consistent under perturbation  
- the commitment does not dissolve naturally  
- the structure becomes part of NDH’s long‑term architecture  

If the structure is fragile or transient, it is not FO‑II.

---

### **Constraint Introduction**  
The event must introduce constraints that guide or limit system behavior.  
These constraints may include:

- required interactions  
- guaranteed outcomes  
- prohibited behaviors  
- standardized procedures  

FO‑II events shape how NDH must behave, not just how it can behave.

---

## ⭐ 3. Secondary Indicators  
These indicators strengthen FO‑II classification but are not required.

### **Cross‑Component Adoption**  
Multiple subsystems begin using the formalized structure.

### **Predictive Reliability**  
The structure enables reliable forecasting of system behavior.

### **Interoperability Enhancement**  
The structure improves compatibility between components.

### **Long‑Term Stability**  
The structure remains relevant across architectural evolution.

---

## ⭐ 4. FO‑II Exclusion Conditions  
A candidate event is **not** FO‑II if any of the following occur:

### **Emergent Origin**  
If the event arises naturally from manifold dynamics, it is FO‑I.

### **Architectural Transformation**  
If the event modifies NDH’s architecture, it is FO‑III.

### **Local Scope**  
If the event affects only a small region of the manifold, it is not FO‑II.

### **Non‑Binding Nature**  
If the event does not impose constraints or commitments, it is not FO‑II.

---

## ⭐ 5. FO‑II Event Lifecycle  
FO‑II events follow a structured lifecycle:

1. **Stabilization** — a concept becomes reliable  
2. **Evaluation** — the concept is assessed for system‑wide value  
3. **Commitment** — the concept is formally adopted  
4. **Definition** — rules, interfaces, or guarantees are established  
5. **Integration** — the structure becomes part of NDH’s architecture  

This lifecycle ensures formalization is deliberate and coherent.
