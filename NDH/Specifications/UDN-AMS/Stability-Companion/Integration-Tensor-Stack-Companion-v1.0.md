### 🌐 Integration Tensor Stack Companion (v1.0)

**Name:** Integration Tensor Stack Companion  
**Tier Span:** Stability Integration (with references to Geometry + Dynamics)  
**Type:** Companion / interpretive specification  
**Purpose:** Explain the meaning, use, and cross‑field interpretation of the Integration Tensor Stack  
\(\mathbb{T}_{\text{Int}} = \{\widetilde{T}^{(\lambda)}, \widetilde{T}^{(\mathcal{R})}, \widetilde{T}^{(\chi)}, \widetilde{T}^{(\mathcal{M})}\}\).

---

### 1. What the integrated tensors are

- **Ṫ(λ) — Integrated Drift Tensor:**  
  **Interpretation:** Net tendency of the system to slide, wander, or settle.  
  **Fields:**  
  - Physics: net drift of state under forces.  
  - Computation: bias and convergence of algorithms.  
  - Psychology: baseline emotional/cognitive drift.  
  - Systems: long‑term operating point of infrastructure.

- **Ṫ(R) — Integrated Curvature Tensor:**  
  **Interpretation:** How strongly the manifold bends experience, load, or flow.  
  **Fields:**  
  - Physics: effective curvature under both structure and motion.  
  - Computation: how strongly state space funnels toward attractors.  
  - Ecology: how landscapes and dynamics shape resource flows.  
  - Systems: stress concentration under real operation.

- **Ṫ(χ) — Integrated Holonomy Tensor:**  
  **Interpretation:** Net “loop behavior”: what happens when you go around cycles.  
  **Fields:**  
  - Physics: phase and gauge‑like behavior under repeated paths.  
  - Computation: feedback loops, recurrent nets, cyclic processes.  
  - Psychology: patterns that repeat—rumination, habits, rituals.  
  - Systems: maintenance cycles, control loops, periodic operations.

- **Ṫ(M) — Integrated Mixed‑Sector Tensor:**  
  **Interpretation:** How sectors co‑influence each other when both structure and motion are present.  
  **Fields:**  
  - Physics: coupled subsystems (thermal–mechanical–electrical).  
  - Computation: modules influencing each other’s stability.  
  - Ecology: trophic and spatial cross‑couplings.  
  - Systems: cross‑domain dependencies (power, network, human ops).

---

### 2. How to read the stack in practice

**Rule 1 — Check signs and magnitudes.**  
- Large positive Ṫ(R) with weak damping → strong funnels, possible lock‑in.  
- Large Ṫ(χ) with non‑suppressed imaginary part → resonance, loops that don’t settle.  
- Large Ṫ(M) → strong cross‑sector coupling; good for coherence, dangerous if unmanaged.

**Rule 2 — Look for coherence across tensors.**  
- Stable systems show **aligned** signs: drift, curvature, holonomy, and mixed‑sector all support the same basin.  
- Unstable systems show **conflicting** tensors: e.g., drift wants to settle, holonomy keeps cycling.

**Rule 3 — Use Integration as the “truth layer.”**  
Geometry can look safe. Dynamics can look safe.  
If Integration says Ṫ(·) is unstable, **believe Integration**—it’s the fused view.

---

### 3. Companion heuristics

- **If Ṫ(λ) is small and Ṫ(R) is large:**  
  System is structurally funnelled but not strongly pulled—slow settling, possible stuckness.

- **If Ṫ(χ) is large and mostly imaginary:**  
  System is loop‑heavy—expect cycles, rituals, oscillations, or feedback chatter.

- **If Ṫ(M) is near zero:**  
  Sectors are decoupled—local stability may be fine, global behavior may be fragmented.

- **If all Ṫ(·) are positive semidefinite and bounded:**  
  You’re in the NDH definition of **globally integrated stability**.
